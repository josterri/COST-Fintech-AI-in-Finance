1. PREFACE
Two years have passed since the last report on the Estonian FinTech sector was prepared (for reference 
see Laidroo, et al., 2021b). Over the years, the Estonian FinTech sector has been characterised by speedy 
development both locally and globally (Laidroo and Avarmaa, 2020; Laidroo et al., 2021a; Tirmaste et al., 
2019). The COVID-19 outbreak created some challenges inside the companies while creating opportunities 
outside. Still, its overall impact on Estonian FinTechs remained modest (Laidroo, et al., 2021b). Geopolitical 
developments following the break-out of the war between Russia and Ukraine in February 2022 have 
increased uncertainties and left a significant mark on the regional economic conditions, impacting the 
everyday activities of most businesses. The startup sector has been further touched by the global decline 
in the availability of funding.  
This report aims to provide an overview of the recent developments influencing the Estonian FinTech 
sector and the main characteristics and challenges of the FinTechs. This report is prepared in cooperation 
between TalTech School of Business and Governance and FinanceEstonia, which is a non-profit 
organisation representing the financial sector. As mapping the FinTech landscape is a challenge, because 
of the difficulties in distinguishing FinTechs from non-FinTechs, we would urge the Estonian FinTechs not 
mentioned in this report to let us know of their existence, so they can provide input for future reports. 
The report is divided into several sections. Section 2 provides the definitions and the overall framework of 
the report. Section 3 presents an overview of the current Estonian FinTech environment with the main 
focus on changes in the legal environment and regulatory sandbox approach in Estonia. Section 4 presents 
an overview of Estonian FinTechs and the results of the survey conducted in spring 2023. Section 5 
concludes the report by presenting the most important findings. 
We greatly appreciate the support provided by the FinanceEstonia FinTech workgroup in contacting the 
FinTechs, and by the FinTechs who participated in the survey. We would like to thank Andrus Alber, Anu 
Müürsepp, Kristen Leppik, Thomas Auväärt, Siiri Tõniste, Kristiina Kubja for their input and feedback on 
this report. We acknowledge the contribution of Bruno Isand, Hedi Radtšenko, Kristiina Pullerits, Mari 
Avarmaa in preparing this report. We would like to thank our colleagues Thomas Ankenbrand, Andreas

regulatory sandbox. We also value the moral support from colleagues involved in COST Action CA19130 –

2. DEFINITION AND FRAMEWORK OF THE STUDY
2.1. Definition of FinTech 
There are numerous definitions of FinTech but no 
consensus about which is the most appropriate. 
In the 2023 report, we rely on the definition by 
EBA (2017) p.4: “technologically enabled 
financial innovation that could result in new 
business models, applications, processes or 
products with an associated material effect on 
financial markets and institutions and the 
provision of financial services”. This means that 
this report continues to cover both startups and 
established firms involved in or supporting the 
provision of financial services. To be included in 
the report, the FinTech companies had to be 
incorporated in Estonia meaning that we are 
omitting all branches (e.g., Wise, Monese). 
 
To capture the developments in the FinTech 
sector more accurately, this report relies on a 
categorisation similar to the one introduced in 
CCAF, World Bank and World Economic Forum 
(2022). The latter report divides FinTechs into 
two broad categories – retail facing and market 
provisioning – which we treat in this report as 
providers of financial services and providers of 
technology and support services. 
 
Providers 
of 
financial 
services 
include 
companies involved in: 
• 
Digital lending – P2P lending, balance 
sheet lending, including invoice trading, 
leasing, consumer credit. 
• 
Digital capital raising – equity-based, 
revenue/profit 
share, 
reward-based, 
donation-based 
crowdfunding, 
real-
estate crowdfunding, ICO platforms. 
• 
Digital banking – fully digital banks, 
providers of banking as a service (BaaS) 
that have obtained a banking licence. 
• 
Digital savings – digital savings solutions, 
savings-as-a-service. 
• 
Digital payments – mobile payments, 
money 
transfers, 
e-money 
issuers, 
points of access, other payment-related 
services. 
• 
Digital asset exchange – trading and 
brokerage services including different 
platforms, exchanges, Bitcoin Teller 
Machines etc. 
• 
Digital custody - digital wallets, key 
management services. 
• 
WealthTech – robo-advisors, social 
trading, personal financial management, 
financial comparison sites.  
• 
InsurTech – insurance-related products 
and services, including digital brokers or 
agents, 
peer-to-peer 
insurance, 
insurance comparison portals etc. 
 
Providers of technology and support services 
include: 
• 
Alternative credit & data analytics – 
credit scoring based on alternative data, 
solutions based on analysis of biometric

•

•

Enterprise technology provisioning –

Compared to the initial categorisations by CCAF, 
World Bank and World Economic Forum (2022), 
we exclude providers of digital accounting 
solutions as these are not specifically targeted at 
providers of financial services. Also, our 
interpretation of some of the sub-categories 
includes some modifications. All companies 
covered by this report had to fall into one of the 
sub-categories which we initially determined in 
the course of desk research based on public data 
in different registries and companies’ web pages. 
For those companies that responded to the 
survey, 
we 
cross-checked 
the 
initial 
categorisations based on the responses.

2.2. Research methods 
This report draws mainly on a survey that was 
carried out in the spring of 2023. The 2023 
survey concentrated, similarly to the 2021 
survey, on the business model characteristics of 
Estonian FinTechs. These were analysed using 
the business model canvas of Osterwalder and 
Pigneur (2010), covering the key activities, key 
resources, value proposition, customer channels 
and segments, and revenue streams that have 
been modified by the value proposition and 
product/service delivery classifications from 
Eickhoff et al. (2017). However, compared to the 
survey 
instrument 
used 
in 
2021, 
some 
improvements were made. Firstly, the business 
model attributes covering technology were 
modified according to Imerman & Fabozzi (2020) 
classification. Secondly, the opinions and 
evaluations of the future outlook and regulative 
changes were adjusted.  
 
In addition, this report includes section 3.2. the 
findings of which are based on the policy 
documents and eight semi-structured interviews 
with key policymakers (from the Ministry of 
Finance, Estonian Financial Supervision and 
Resolution Authority, and the Bank of Estonia) 
and an industry representative. This preparation 
of this part of the report has been supported by 
the Estonian Research Council grant PRG1125.

3. ESTONIAN FINTECH ENVIRONMENT
3.1. Changes in the legal 
environment 
By Mari-Liis Kukk 
 
As previous editions of this report (Laidroo et al., 
2021b; Tirmaste et al., 2019) have noted, the 
regulatory landscape for financial services in 
Estonia is based mostly on EU-wide agreements, 
as the union is seeking to ultimately achieve a 
single market for financial services. Harmonizing 
regulatory approaches among Member States is 
one important step in achieving that goal. 
However, there are also some local aspects, 
which must be focused on separately. As such, 
changes to the legal environment are covered in 
two parts. Firstly, Section 3.1.1. describes the 
FinTech-related 
changes 
currently 
being 
implemented in the EU law. Then, Section 3.1.2. 
discusses new additions to the local Estonian 
legislative framework. 
3.1.1. Changes in European Union Law 
As also mentioned in the previous edition of the 
report, EU regulatory ambitions surrounding 
innovative financial services specifically arise 
from the digital finance package, which was 
released in September 2020 and included several 
proposals for introducing new regulations. 
Although compared to the previous edition of 
the report, the digital finance package has not 
led to any significant new regulatory proposals, 
the initial texts have by now all gone through the 
legislative processes and have entered into force. 
The Regulation on European Crowdfunding 
Service Providers (ECSPR) entered into force on 
November 10, 2020, and was applied exactly 12 
months later, as expected. However, the initial 
expectation was that all existing crowdfunding 
platforms, which had previously been licensed 
under national rules, should have been 
transitioned to the new EU-wide license by 
November 10, 2022. However, on July 12, 2022, 
the EU Commission proposed to extend that 
deadline by 12 months, based on an assessment 
by the European Securities and Markets 
Authority that without an extension, several 
competent authorities may not be able to 
process the applications of service providers in 
due time, and large disruptions of crowdfunding 
markets could occur. (Regulation 2022/1988) 
 
The next regulatory development from the 
digital finance package to enter into force was 
the regulation of a pilot regime for market 
infrastructures based on distributed ledger 
technology (DLT), commonly known as the DLT 
Pilot regime, which entered into force on June 
23, 2022, and became applicable on March 23, 
2023. Unlike other regulations in the package, 
the aim of the DLT Pilot is not so much to apply 
regulatory demands to novel business models, 
but instead to create a special EU-wide sandbox-
esque regime for testing the use of DLT for the 
trading and post-trading of crypto-assets that 
qualify as regulated financial instruments. The 
reasoning behind creating this regime was that 
the regulatory treatment of such instruments is 
rather uncertain, and there exist certain 
regulatory obstacles in traditional rules regarding 
financial services that significantly hamper the 
use of DLT for financial market infrastructures. 
Under the regime, which all Member States have 
to apply directly, firms wishing to establish 
market infrastructures using DLT can apply for 
certain exemptions while still being able to offer 
their services publicly. The EU is hoping that 
allowing such experimentations will allow 
regulators to learn about the nuances of applying 
DLT, and lead to a permanent EU regulatory 
regime for such markets being established. 
(European Commission, 2020) 
 
The Digital Operational Resilience Act (DORA), 
which aims to mitigate risks pertaining to cyber-
attacks, and put into place safeguards that would 
help all financial service providers ensure that 
risks 
involving 
the 
use 
of 
information 
communication 
technologies 
would 
be 
sufficiently accounted for, entered into force on 
January 16, 2023. However, its application date is 
set at January 17, 2025, with the standard 
implementation period of 12 months being 
doubled because of the high level of complexity 
involved in meeting the standards (Moek & 
Huijpen, 2023). Notably, unlike other proposals 
in the digital finance package, DORA is not 



 
 
 
 
9 
 
targeted at any specific new financial service but 
is instead intended to cover novel risks arising 
from the increased digitalisation of financial 
services that affect all providers. What is more, 
the scope of DORA may also extend beyond 
financial 
services, 
as 
critical 
third-party 
technology providers will also have to meet 
certain requirements (KPMG, n.d.).  
 
The fourth, and currently the youngest digital 
finance package regulatory development is the 
Markets in Crypto-assets (MiCA) regulation, 
which entered into force on 29 June 2023. The 
regulation includes disclosure requirements for 
the issuance, offering and admission to trading of 
so-called non-stablecoin crypto-assets while 
imposing 
stricter 
requirements, 
including 
authorisation requirements to crypto-assets 
deemed as stablecoins, which for MiCA purposes 
have been classified as either e-money tokens or 
asset-referenced tokens, depending on the 
specific 
setup. 
MiCA 
also 
introduces 
authorisation requirements for the service 
providers that facilitate transactions with crypto-
assets, listing eight different types of services 
that will have to comply with both universal and 
activity-specific 
requirements. 
Importantly, 
MiCA does not cover truly decentralized services, 
nor does it regulate unique, non-fungible tokens, 
which cannot be directly interchangeable with 
another token. Furthermore, as noted above, 
crypto-assets that fall under the definition of 
financial instruments will remain regulated 
under previously established rules. The timeline 
for MiCA application is set out in the regulation, 
with rules pertaining to stablecoins becoming 
applicable 12 months after MiCA entres into 
force, and the rest of MiCA applying 6 months 
later (Regulation 2023/1114). 
 
While not specifically named under the digital 
finance package, the EU has kept proceeding 
with a separate regulation on the information 
accompanying transfers of funds and certain 
crypto-assets (TfR) in tandem with MiCA. 
Fundamentally, this regulation introduces the so-
called “Travel Rule”, familiar from traditional 
financial services, to crypto asset service 
providers as well. The “Travel Rule” relates to the 
information that is passed along about the 
parties involved when executing electronic 
transactions, enabling to trace the movement of 
funds and identify all parties starting from the 
source to the final destination. This information 
can thus be used by law enforcement, for 
instance when investigating cases of money 
laundering and terrorist financing. The TfR will 
enter into force on the same date as MiCA. 
 
Aside from regulations directly targeted at 
financial services, FinTech companies may also 
have to consider other EU-wide regulations that 
have been recently introduced, such as the 
Digital Services Act (DSA) and the Digital Markets 
Act (DMA), which were both published in the EU 
Official Journal in October 2022 and entered into 
force on November 16, 2022, and November 1, 
2022, respectively. The two acts work in unison 
and aim to provide better protection of the 
fundamental rights of customers online, combat 
illegal content and establish a level playing field 
when it comes to very large platforms. (European 
Commission, n.d.) Financial services are also set 
to be considered in the scope of the Artificial 
Intelligence Act (AI Act), which was first 
proposed in April 2021 and could be adopted in 
2023. The AI Act will classify artificial intelligence 
applications based on their presumed risks, with 
some applications being completely prohibited, 
others required to go through a pre-approval 
process and some having to disclose certain 
information aimed at increasing transparency 
(European Parliament, 2023). The EU is also 
currently working on harmonized rules for the 
use and access of data generated in EU markets, 
as first proposed via the Data Act in February 
2022 (European Commission, 2022). As this 
regulation aims to encourage the use of data to 
provide innovative financial services, it could 
have profound effects on financial markets. 
However, a certain draft of the Data Act has also 
been met with criticism, notably by DLT 
companies, as certain provisions may be in 
conflict with how smart contracts stored on 
blockchains operate (Schickler, 2023).

the Money Laundering and Terrorist Financing

was also extensively amended in March 2022, 
with a focus on tightening requirements set for 
CASPs. For instance, the amendments required 
CASPs to submit a business plan when applying 
for authorisation, there was a significant increase

subjected to external audit requirements. The 
amendments also included a version of the 
Travel Rule, under which certain identifiable 
information regarding the initiator of the 
transaction 
must 
be 
collected 
(Money

arguably likened the requirements applicable for 
Estonian CASPs under local AML rules to those 
that will apply to CASPs EU-wide under the MiCA 
regulation. 
 
In addition to regulation, the Ministry of Finance 
and the Ministry of Economic Affairs and 
Communications are working on the Estonian 
FinTech Strategy 2023+. This document aims at 
determining the vision for FinTech in Estonia and 
setting targets for the coming years to overcome 
the main challenges of the sector. It is expected 
to be completed by the end of 2023. 
 
 
3.2. Estonia’s cautious approach 
towards regulatory sandboxes 
By Egert Juuse, Ringa Raudla 
 
The adoption of a regulatory sandbox (RS) for 
FinTech companies has been on the agenda of 
public institutions in Estonia for a few years, but 
to this date, it has not materialized, contrary to 
Latvia and Lithuania. Even though the first official 
steps were taken in 2019 (EBRD, 2019), when 
EBRD and the Ministry of Finance launched a 
project to support the creation of a RS in Estonia, 
Estonia has opted for the innovation hub format 
that was launched in 2021. Despite long-lasting 
discussions, several factors can be highlighted to 
explain why the launch of RS in Estonia has been 
delayed  
 
In a nutshell these could be summarized as 
follows:  
• 
ambivalence about the idea, the concept 
and the expected outcomes of the RS 
tool 
among 
policymakers 
and 
supervisors;  
• 
lack of interest from the political level 
about the RS; 
• 
missing mandates and legal grounds, 
and unsupportive institutional set-up of 
the 
central 
banking, 
competition 
promotion and supervision functions; 
• 
conservative 
and 
cautious 
characteristics 
of 
public 
sector 
organizations, 
and 
lacking 
internal 
capacities; 
• 
unsupportive financing arrangements of 
the FSA activities and suboptimality of 
the RS tool in a small state context;  
• 
Innovation Hub as a pre-condition and 
existing alternative substitute(s) for 
sandboxing (e.g. incubators);  
• 
the EU’s initiatives and regulatory 
landscape as ineffective triggers and 
enablers for temporary relaxation of 
adopted rules;  
• 
lack of demand for a RS from the existing 
FinTech sector.  
 
Overall, discussions about the adoption of a RS in 
Estonia have lasted for the last seven years and 
even though there has not been an active 



 
 
 
 
11 
 
opposition to the instrument, it has not been 
realized to this day. Most of the arguments 
against the creation of RS could be ascribed to 
small state peculiarities such as lacking financial 
resources, insufficient human capital, insufficient 
demand, low return on investment from a RS, 
and the availability of alternative and more 
optimal solutions. Other deterring factors have 
been revolving around legal uncertainty, lacking 
mandates and the missing political demand to 
deal with the topic. At the same time, one cannot 
neglect the role of idiosyncratic elements such as 
money laundering scandals of the Nordic banks 
and the failed regulatory ‘experiment’ with 
crypto service providers in the mid-2010s that 
fed a risk-averse and conservative stance among 
civil servants in the Ministry of Finance and the 
FSA.

4.1. Overview of Estonian FinTech 
companies 
By Triinu Tapver, Laivi Laidroo 
The discussion in this section is based on the list 
of FinTechs compiled by the authors of this 
report using data retrieved from the Crunchbase, 
Startup Estonia, FinTech Baltic, Key Capital, and 
FinanceEstonia databases as of December 2022. 
This data was complemented with companies 
identified amongst those that have received 
permits and licenses required for the provision of 
financial services in Estonia. To ensure a uniform 
definition of FinTechs, we applied the definitions 
given in Section 1. In addition, the list of FinTechs

count of FinTechs has increased less due to exits

had

14

unique paperwork were mentioned. As this 
survey probably reached more active crypto 
service providers, the actual adoption level and 
potential for the whole population of Estonian 
FinTechs is likely to remain at a significantly 
lower level.  
On a scale of 1 (not appropriate at all) to 7 (very 
appropriate), 
these 
seven 
respondents 
evaluated also the sufficiency of the application 
field of the crypto asset regulation proposal. The 
average score was 4.4, indicating a rather 
positive opinion. However, the respondents 
were much more critical about how well the 
same regulation reduces risks in the sector. The 
average score was 2.8 which indicates a very 
modest reduction of risks. These results show 
that although some aspects of the regulation are 
viewed positively, there remains room for 



24 
improvement. The same tendencies were 
observed already in the 2021 survey, with the 
same scores being 4.2 and 3.9, respectively. 
When asked to highlight areas that needed 
further 
regulations, 
57% 
of 
respondents 
mentioned providing credit in exchange for 
crypto-assets, 42% mentioned the NFTs, and 
decentralized service providers and only one 
respondent marked storing crypto-assets for 
earning interest (staking). Two respondents 
provided no input for further regulation areas 
and firmly stated that no further regulation is 
needed.  
On the negative side, 71% of respondents, 
indicated that the crypto asset regulation 
approval would lead to the closure of a 
significant portion of Estonian crypto companies. 
28% of the respondents also believed that some 
crypto companies in Estonia will either 
consolidate or acquire a license from another EU 
country. Only one respondent thought that 
companies with licenses from abroad will 
acquire new licenses based on the new 
regulation from Estonia. This indicates that the 
market 
participants 
believe 
a 
further 
consolidation of the Estonian crypto market 
which has anyhow been through significant 
negative developments during recent years with 
the cancellation of the majority of the VASP 
licenses.

5. CONCLUSION
This report aimed to provide an overview of the 
recent developments influencing the Estonian 
FinTech sector and the main characteristics and 
challenges of the FinTechs. A survey of FinTech 
companies was used to draw some general 
conclusions. 
First, the economic impact of FinTech is gradually 
increasing. Although the growth in the number 
of FinTechs has decelerated, compared to the 
end of 2020, the count of FinTechs has increased 
by 23%, reaching 264 firms by the end of 2022. 
The deceleration of growth arises as the growth 
of FinTechs by 126 companies has been 
counterbalanced with an exit of 77 former 
FinTech list constituents. As of the end of 2021, 
the total assets of FinTechs stood already at 2.24 
billion euros, 1.2 times the numbers in 2019. 
Total 
income 
generated 
in 
2022 
was 
approximately 400 million euros, an increase of 
70% compared to 2020 and survey respondents 
were expecting on average a 46% increase in 
income on a year-by-year basis in 2023. As of the 
end of 2022 4th quarter, the total employee count 
was 2529 which is 27% greater than in 2020. Still, 
30% of survey respondents did indicate that no 
change in employment in 2023 is expected and 
compared to the 2021 survey, the proportions of 
respondents indicating an increase in the 
workforce has decreased by 14 percentage 
points. This does indicate that the economic 
conditions are likely to influence also the 
activities of FInTechs, however, their impact on 
income may remain less evident.  
Second, the field remains dominated by 
companies involved in digital asset exchange that 
account for 33% of all Estonian FinTechs despite 
the significant decline in the number of 
companies having a valid VASP license. This 
reflects the impact of past decisions regarding 
licensing, however, given the coming changes 
with the MiCA adoption, the share of this FinTech 
category is likely to decrease, as estimated also 
by survey respondents. Despite the strong 
presence of FinTechs focusing on crypto assets, 
the Estonian FinTech space remains diverse with 
a strong presence of FinTechs involved in digital 
lending, enterprise technology provisioning, 
digital payments WealthTech and others.  
Third, the business models of Estonian FinTechs 
have not changed significantly. Innovative 
technologies in the form of blockchain, big data, 
AI etc. are applied and, in many cases, also 
outsourced from outside the company. Value 
proposition remains dominated by transparency 
(e.g., improving access to timely and sufficient 
information) and monetary value (e.g., financial 
gain for the customer). The majority of FinTechs 
have continued to operate both in Estonia and 
internationally, providing mainly business-to-
business or business-to-customer services. 
FinTech services are delivered mainly through 
web applications and APIs with most of the 
income coming through commission fees. 
Fourth, entrepreneurs’ location choices have not 
changed 
significantly. 
Local 
FinTech 
entrepreneurs prefer Estonia due to familiarity 
with local conditions and stronger networks. Still, 
given that nearly a third of respondents had 
considered relocating their business, poor access 
to customers and regulatory challenges may 
force 
companies 
to 
select 
alternative 
destinations or even re-consider their initial 
location decisions over time.  
Fifth, the greatest challenges for FinTechs lie in 
finding customers, expansion to foreign markets, 
economic conditions and access to finance. 
Compared to 2021, the concerns had most 
strongly increased in the area of access to 
finance and cost of labour, reflecting the recent 
developments in the macroeconomy and global 
venture capital markets. Interestingly, these 
developments had somewhat decreased the 
relative importance of regulatory concerns, 
especially 
amongst 
FinTechs 
from 
fields 
unrelated to crypto-assets. 
Sixth, when considering expanding their business 
abroad, FinTechs do view their relationship with 
regulators more positively compared to the 2021 
survey. A greater obstacle is perceived in the 
form of regulative restrictions to IT solutions, 



 
 
 
 
26 
 
collecting and checking data and lack of contacts 
and networks abroad. 
 
Seventh, FinTechs consider the Estonian FinTech 
sector to be competitive on an average level. The 
greatest development potential is seen in cloud-
based systems, web3 environment, and support 
services for financial sector companies. Still, 
when ranking the factors that could enhance the 
Estonian FinTech sector, regulations and tax 
reliefs emerge on top. On the positive side, 
compared to 2021, the respondents view 
cooperation with relevant authorities more 
positively.  
 
Respondents indicated that they would like to 
see Estonian regulations more in line with similar 
innovative European countries. More specific 
regulatory aspects that would deserve attention, 
based on the opinion of FinTechs, were the 
following: 
• 
Improvement of remote identification 
and online verification outside of 
European Economic Area countries. 
• 
Some sort of licencing in areas that do 
not currently require licencing to 
broaden the business opportunities for 
Estonian FinTechs (e.g., factoring). 
• 
Improving 
the 
investment 
account 
system 
to 
make 
it 
available 
for 
investment companies. 
• 
Making the regulatory environment 
multilingual. 
• 
Making debt collection faster. 
• 
Creating a positive credit register. 
• 
Allowing long-term investment products 
by the employer at the same terms as 
employer’s option programmes. 
• 
Introducing social tax ceiling. 
• 
Improving investors’ possibilities for 
opening Estonian bank accounts. 
 
Overall, the Estonian FinTech sector remains 
strong and if the challenges can be properly 
handled, Estonia would remain an attractive 
location for FinTechs with global ambitions.
